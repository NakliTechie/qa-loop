State of Remote
Work, 2026.

Five years after the great dispersal, distributed work has stopped being a story about where people sit — and become a story about how organizations actually run. This year's data, drawn from 18,400 knowledge workers across 47 countries, finds a labor market that has quietly finished arguing with itself.

The conversation about remote work is over. Not because anyone won the argument, but because the argument stopped being interesting. In 2026, distributed work is no longer a perk, a posture, or a pandemic hangover — it is, for roughly seventy percent of knowledge work, simply the way the work gets done.

What's changed since our 2024 report is less the amount of remote work — that number has moved only four points in two years — and more the quality of it. Organizations have stopped running two parallel operating systems, one for the office and one for everywhere else. The async stack has matured. Compensation bands have been regionalized and then, in many cases, de-regionalized again. Managers have, on average, finally learned to write things down.

The four charts that follow track the shape of this settled landscape: how work arrangements have distributed themselves across quarters, what has happened to measured productivity, where employees say they are thriving (and where they aren't), and the geography of where the world's remote workers actually sit.

Read together, they tell a story of stabilization rather than revolution. The most striking finding isn't in any single chart — it's that the variance between quarters has fallen to its lowest level since we began tracking. The remote-work question, in other words, has stopped being a question.

§ 01 Work arrangement

The hybrid majority holds firm.

Hybrid arrangements stabilized at 42% of knowledge work in 2025 and have not budged since. Fully remote work crept up four points over two years — modest, but the only category still climbing. On-site work, once the default, is now a plurality, not a majority.

§ 02 Productivity index

The productivity gap stopped closing.

Self-reported productivity, indexed to January 2024, has risen across all arrangements — but the remote cohort has widened, not narrowed, its lead. The "productivity penalty" of working from home, much discussed in 2023, has not materialized in the data.

§ 03 Satisfaction dimensions

Different work, different winners.

When employees rate six dimensions of work life, no arrangement wins on everything. Remote workers dominate focus and autonomy; on-site workers lead on collaboration and career growth. Hybrid, the compromise, finishes second on five of six — which is, perhaps, why it remains the plurality.

§ 04 Geographic distribution

Where the remote workers are.

North America still hosts the largest share of the world's remote knowledge workforce, but its lead is shrinking. The most consequential shift since 2024 is the rise of South Asia and Latin America — together now home to nearly a quarter of remote workers, up from 17% two years ago.

Methodology

How we read the room.

The Distributed Review Workforce Survey is conducted quarterly across a rotating panel of 18,400 knowledge workers in 47 countries. Respondents are recruited through a stratified sample weighted by role, seniority, and region. The productivity index combines monthly self-assessment (60% weight) with manager-reviewed output metrics (40% weight), indexed to a January 2024 baseline of 100.

Satisfaction dimensions are scored on a 1–10 scale across six categories: work-life balance, autonomy, collaboration, career growth, focus time, and wellbeing. The geographic distribution figure reflects the primary residence of respondents who report working remotely at least three days per week.

The full dataset and questionnaires are available to subscribers. Margin of error for the headline figures is ±1.3 percentage points at the 95% confidence level.
